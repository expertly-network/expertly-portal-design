# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**Seekers**: CFOs, General Counsel, and senior executives at companies who need verified finance or legal counsel for a specific matter (M&A, transfer pricing, cross-border compliance, IP, capital markets). They are time-poor, risk-aware, and have been burned by referrals that led to junior associates or generalists. They need to find the right person fast, with confidence.

**Practitioners**: Senior finance and legal professionals (partners, former Big-4, senior in-house counsel) who want to be discoverable, build a reputation, and take on selective advisory work. They care deeply about the calibre of the network they're associated with.

Both audiences arrive from professional referrals or word of mouth. Neither responds to hard sales copy. Both need to feel, within seconds, that Expertly was built by and for people like them.

## Product Purpose

Expertly is a curated, verified network of senior finance and legal practitioners. It replaces cold outreach, LinkedIn rabbit holes, and firm referrals that lead nowhere. Seekers can search by practice area, jurisdiction, and rate; practitioners get a verified profile and direct inbound from qualified buyers.

Success looks like: a seeker books a consultation with the right expert within minutes of arriving on the platform; a practitioner sees that their peers on the network are at their level or above.

## Brand Personality

**Authoritative. Curated. Collegial.**

The brand feels like a highly selective professional institution — the kind that doesn't need to advertise. It has the understated authority of Sequoia Capital or a16z: confident enough to let quality speak without ornamentation. It is warm because practitioners know each other's world; it is exclusive because that's the point. It is never cold, never corporate-committee, never startup-casual.

## Anti-references

- **Generic legal directories** (Avvo, Martindale-Hubbell): cluttered, SEO-driven, low-trust, commoditised
- **Big-4 consulting firm websites** (Deloitte, PwC, EY): committee-designed, lifeless, speak to procurement not practitioners
- **VC-funded SaaS startups** (Notion/Linear aesthetic clones): san-francisco-generic, no domain gravitas, too "product"
- **Freelancer/gig marketplaces** (Upwork, Fiverr): race-to-bottom pricing signals, commoditisation of expertise

## Design Principles

1. **Domain fluency first** — Every word, section, and visual decision should feel authored by someone who knows finance and law. Copy that could describe any SaaS product fails this test. Specificity is credibility.

2. **Restraint is the signal** — Premium networks don't shout. Quality is communicated by what's absent: no hero stats dressed up as metrics, no gradient badges, no cluttered social proof. One precise detail earns more trust than ten features listed in bullet points.

3. **The club, not the directory** — Every design choice should reinforce that both the seeker and the practitioner are accessing something selective. Membership is earned; discovery is privileged.

4. **Institutional confidence** — The visual register should feel like Sequoia Capital's site or a tier-1 investment bank's communications: clean grids, serious typography, purposeful use of space. The site should not try hard to impress; it should simply be impressive.

5. **Warm authority** — Not cold/corporate. Practitioners are peers. The tone and visual warmth should signal a collegial professional community, not a bureaucracy or a marketplace.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA as the baseline. The audience is global (20+ jurisdictions) and senior — do not assume young eyes or fast connections. Body text contrast must exceed 4.5:1. Reduced motion must be honoured. The site must be fully navigable by keyboard.
